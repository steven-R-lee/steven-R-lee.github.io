1. when I got blocked on a simple problem

what was the problem?

I wanted to delete a folder with several files in it, on my computer. However, I was new to the computer's operating system and I wasn't confident about issuing the correct commands to my computer in order to delete the unwanted folder and the files sitting within it.

what was the problem solving techniques did i use?

I used 3 problem sovling techniques to solve the problem:

  1. Tried Something: explored if there was a simple command which I could apply to delete the unwanted file - NO!
  2. Googled the Problem: conducted an internet search to discover if anyone else had come across the same problem and resolved it - YES!
  3. Asked a Coach for help: asked a coach for advice and confirmation to use the "rm -rf" command line in order to avoid doing something that might have caused further issues - ADVICE TAKEN and PROBLEM SOLVED!
how did I feel throughout the process?

I felt apprehensive at trying something new, but not fearful to the state where I froze. I always knew my problem had a straight-forward solution and that I merely required some re-assurance.

what did I learn?

The coaches at EDA have seen the overwhelming majority of problems before, and can offer the guidance and advice for me to ultimately solve my own problems.

on how confident you feel on using the problam solving techniques and processes

Pseudocode: I love this technique and it is often the first one I reach for when faced with a really challenging problem. It gives direction and clarity to problem solving. It provides a skeleton and placeholders to place the code.

Trying Something: I also love this technique. I've found that it is usually pretty inmprobable, especially as a beginner, to mess a task completely up. And that having a go, and trying somethings - different approaches - usually leads to furher learning opportunities and eventually deeper understanding of the task that was causing the problem.

Rubber Duck Method: Nope! This method to solving problem doesn't work that effectively for me, largely because there is no feedback-loop.

Reading Error Messages: The Error Messages often thrown-up by the computer can be somewhat cryptic, but they can often offer a good starting point for solving a problem.

Console Logging: I find this technique useful as it makes visible the invisible of what is going on behind the scenes. Again, they can offer a good starting point for solving a problem.

Googling: Undoubtedly a quick and powerful techinique, but I find it can offer only superficial offering to the learning process. I need to hear it from the human voice.

Asking Your Peers For Help: I'm never shy to ask for help. Questions aways offer the Questioner the best opportunity to learn. They can also help cement the knowledge of the Respondent. A kind of mutual benefit for both parties

Asking Coaches For Help: Ditto.

Improving Your Process With Reflection: I subscribe to Journaling which makes me reflect on my learning experiences. I have found reflection prepares me for future challenges.
